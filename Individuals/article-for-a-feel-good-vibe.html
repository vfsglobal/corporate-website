• Cravats & Hats

      Did you know cravats originated in Croatia? I didn’t either; till perchance I stumbled inside the Oktogon passageway, lined with shopping temptations. Croats take immense pride for having invented a type of necktie of which Croata is the most famous brand exported worldwide. Inside Oktogon I also chanced upon ‘Hats by Kobali’, opened in 1974 and now another European favourite in Monte Carlo, Paris and London. “Every single hat tells a story – it looks for a head and is beautiful only if it finds one,” says the famous milliner who represents the fourth generation of hatmakers in her family.

    • The Cathedral

      Sing Ave Maria! At the Cathedral of the Assumption of the Holy Virgin Mary. In the front courtyard, the Holy Virgin Mary’s golden statue is perched on a high column with four gilded angels at the base. The Cathedral itself is the tallest and most monumental Neo-Gothic style sacral building in the entire southern Alps region! In this historic setting, the cathedral’s two elegant bell towers can be seen from a radius of ten kilometres away. Severely damaged in an 1880 earthquake, the cathedral’s restoration stands testimony to the robust health of Croatia’s Catholic faith. The Treasury here contains priceless works of religious art and objects on display.

    • Stone Gate

      A 10-minute walk away from the Cathedral, on ancient cobblestoned streets, meander gently up a hill through a Stone Gate to a painting of the Holy Mother Mary and infant Jesus. Pray. Make a wish. This Chapel was built to commemorate the survival of these medieval walls. On its 200th anniversary, this Stone Gate and open-air Chapel were officially declared a pilgrimage site.

    • St. Mark’s Square

      The sense of having stepped back in time at least 500 years continues with the sight of a sign which indicates it to be Europe’s second-oldest pharmacy. It still sells ancient potions and herbs. I almost imagine toothless grinning witches going bubble, bubble, toil and trouble! Ten steps ahead, your hard climb is rewarded by yet another visual delight. St Mark’s Church stands as a iconic monument, covered with a roof of ceramic glazed tiles that glisten brightly in the Sun and can be seen sparkling colorfully from miles away.

    • Mending Broken Hearts

      For such a small city, Zagreb has many museums. Some of them very quirky. From great artists and sports stars to illusions and torture chambers, there’s a museum for every swank or spank. Check out the most innovative European Museum winner– Museum of Broken Relationships. Packed with tourists from across the world, I saw nubile nymphets from Japan with tears in their eyes, young besotted couples sighing away while I was sorely tempted to ROFL. From matchboxes to wedding gowns, from high heeled shoes to anguished letters, from a pharmaceutical bowl(!) to an axe that was used to destroy an ex-lover’s furniture one day at a time(!!), this Museum is a collection of material objects that signify the death of a relationship and ergo, broken hearts.

    • Foodies Fads

      Pizzas by the slice. Corn cobs sold steamingly sizzled by street vendors. Big Burgers at Rouge Maria restaurant situated inside an ex-light bulb factory! The Basement wine bar where over 90 Croatian wines are served by the glass, accompanied by a super selection of Croatian cheeses. Sweet tooth? VINCEK is a Zagreb institution, jam-packed night and day. We pigged out on almond and dense dark chocolate, nougat and pistachio ice creams, and organic tea.

    • 24 X 7 Party Zone

      Our new friend Tihana describes Zagreb as “Europe’s largest open-air, 24x7 feel good zone”. Misplaced pride or hyperbole? Tkalciceva (pronounced Kalchi cheva) street validates her claim. Over 200 years ago, a bubbling brook powered the city’s thriving floor mills in this area. Then came the paper, liqueur and leather factories, their effluents turning the pristine waters poisonous. In 1910, the city fathers diverted the stream and built a cobblestone street. Scores of pubs, bars, cafes and restaurants opened . Feeding tourists from richer European cities. Now it’s a vibey, trancey avenue crowded by shiny happy people (think drunk!) quaffing barrels of beer and magnums of vodka, while watching the world walk by. Champs Elysees it is not. Friendly, fun, foodie it is. Mostly great value-for-money.
