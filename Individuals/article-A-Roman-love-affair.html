• Why is Rome called the Eternal city? No other city in the world presents 2,500 years of architecture, art, music, food and wine like Rome does! Ancient Rome merges seamlessly into Rome of the Popes while holding a bond with the modern-day Rome. It’s a living, breathing museum. The traffic is chaotic, so metro transport is ideal, it delivers you effortlessly to all the spectacular sights spread out. Once you are in the open air, please walk. When you get tired, drink a cappuccino, gobble up a pizza slice, eat a gelato, or sip an aperitivo like Romans do.

      Day 1

      Take the underground metro to the Colosseo station. As soon as you emerge out into the bright sunlight, your vision will be captivated by one of the greatest remaining monuments of ancient Rome. Originally called The Flavian Amphitheatre, it took nearly a 100 years and three emperors to build what the world gapes at with open mouth – the Colosseum. Amongst the thousands of statistics, let me give you just two to present the genius of its builders. First, this gigantic circle of stone was built on marshy ground after draining the lake. Second, the design of this free standing structure of stone, brick and marble (190 metres in diameter) was to enable at least 60,000 excitable Romans to enter, find their seats, get entertained and finally leave by 80 exits without killing each other! For nearly 400 years, citizens were entertained in what author Charles Dickens described as “the most impressive, most stately, grand, majestic, mournful sights.”

      You must go inside the Colosseo amphitheatre and climb up those ancient stairs. It was the first permanent amphitheatre to be raised in Rome and the most impressive arena the classical world had yet seen. Today, it’s the most visited monument in all of Italy!

      Next, walk back towards the Colosseo metro station, the starting point of a large avenue called Via Del Fori Imperiali. As you saunter along this Imperial Road, on your left are the ruins of the Foro Romani (Roman Forum), birthplace of ancient Rome around the 8th century BC. Now excavated and restored, here lie the landmark ruins of Temple of Vespasian and Temple of Saturn, the column of Phocas, Temple of Antonios and Faustina plus scores of columns. To see these glories of Roman Forum (Foro Romani) is to understand the larger-than-life attitude of the ancient Romans and how they inspired the grandiosity of hundreds of architectural marvels.

      So, in a straight line on the Via Imperiali, the Colosseum lies on the left, Foro Romani in the middle and on the right is the Capitoline Hill (the smallest of the seven hills of ancient Rome) crowned by Michaelangelo’s magnificient Campidoglio (Capitoline Museums Complex). Climb up the imposing cordonata staircase to the terrace from where you get an exceptional view of the Roman Forum and the Palatine Hill, all of it dating back to 2500 years! Got an hour to spare? Visit the Capitoline Museum, which includes the world’s oldest public museum, displays artifacts (statuary, sarcophagi, jewellery) and has rooms teeming with ancient sculptures and mosaics.

      If you’re hungry after all this traipsing around, just head to the Terrace Café on top of the Capitoline Museum to dig into sandwiches and a beer and get rewarded with a 360-degree view of Rome! Two cafes here– Emmanuel’s Food and Terrazza Caffarelli, serve Italian fast food and Mediterranean cuisines. Tasty Pizzas and Salads are on offer at Vinando and Edoardo II, while Gelateria Vacanze’s Ice Creams are to die for. Yummy!

    • Day 2

      More of ancient Rome’s must-see sights on the agenda. Alight at the Flaminio metro station and enter the vast beautiful square of Piazza del Popolo. About 300 years ago, it was the smart meeting place for evening carriage drives and carnivals. As you enter this vast Piazza, it’s impossible to miss the Church of Santa Maria del Popolo to your left. A peep inside reveals the fabled artist Caravaggio’s paintings of ‘Conversion of St. Paul’ and ‘Crucifixion of St. Peter’. The main altar of the church is baroque and frames the famous Madonna. Next door to the church is the Leonardo da Vinci Museum, another must-visit.

      Looking across the beauty of the Piazza are the twin churches of S. Maria di Monte Santo and S. Maria de Miracoli. They straddle three main avenues - Via Ripetta, Via del Corso and Via del Babuino. Take the last one and within five minutes you arrive at the breath-taking Spanish Steps at Piazza di Spagna. Built in 1726, this is the most-famous street staircase in the world! In front is the Barcaccia Fountain where all tourists first congregate and then climb the staircase which has three landings and thus is called Trinita dei Monti.

      Next? The Trevi Fountain. How to get there? Easy. Facing the Spanish Steps, turn right on the street called Via Del Traforo which meets up with Main Avenue called Via Del Corso. You will see the signs indicating where to head to arrive at Italy’s largest and most famous Baroque fountain. The centrepiece in the Trevi Fountain is the Greek Sea God Oceanus, accompanied by sea-horses and Tritons who are half men and half mermen. Factoid: An estimated 3,000 euros are thrown into the fountain each day!

      The Pantheon is a 10-minute walk from Trevi – it is the very essence of Rome, with its magnificent proportions still fascinating architects across the world! It was first built as a temple dedicated to all the gods of pagan Rome between A.D 118 and 125.

      Imagine spending an entire evening, drinking wine, eating dinner whilst getting entertained by musicians, dancers, mime artists and even getting a portrait of yourself in the world’s most celebrated rectangle. Piazza Navona awaits your presence for an unforgettable evening. Adorned with fine Renaissance and Baroque buildings of the 16th Century, the breath-taking magnificence of the Piazza is enhanced by an Obelisk and not one but three fountains. At the Southern end, the Fountain of the Moor features an Ethiopian fighting with a dolphin. To the North, the Fountain of Neptune features sea figures playing in the basin, sea horses, cherubs, dolphins, and Nereids (sea nymphs). In the centre stands the glorious Fountain of the Four Rivers. The fountain consists of four figures, each representing a river: Nile, Ganges, Danube and Rio della Plata.

    • Day 3

      Here’s the link between the 2000-year-old ancient and 500-year-old modern Rome. Both the Castel Sant’ Angelo and the St Peter’s Piazza of the Vatican are on the left side of the River Tiber that snakes through Rome. From the side of Piazza Narona, walk to the river where among many bridges, stands the Ponte Sant Angelo. The three central arches of the bridge are ancient remains of another bridge dating back to second century AD. With Bernini’s statues of angels, the Ponte St. Angelo leads you straight to another glory of ancient Rome - Castel Sant’ Angelo.

      Under this 20-meter high massive circular edifice lies Hadrian’s Tomb. Once inside, take a look at the many rooms containing a collection of arms from the Bronze Age to modern times. Other rooms are former residences of various Popes and their internal decors with splendid frescoes and paintings. A narrow staircase will lead you to the terrace which gives you a splendid 360-degree view of Rome.

      Avid tourists can take a walk on the splendid avenue called Via Della Conciliazone from CSA to the world famous St. Peter’s Basilica of the Vatican. The latter’s spectacular architecture arises like a surreal dream as you walk nearer.

      Inside the vast circular Piazza surrounded by four files of column that soar sky high are also an Obelisk and two beautiful fountains. And there it is, the grand St Peter’s Basilica from whose upper terrace, popes have always delivered their sermons, speeches and blessings. Visitors are allowed to climb the stairway to Heaven for a considerable fee.

      • Travel Tips

      • Carry cash: Italy still largely functions as a cash economy, so be sure to carry plenty with you. Coins are especially welcome at cafés and small shops. Most stores and large restaurants will process cards, but don’t traditionally split the bill so cash is always useful, especially if you’re travelling in a group.
      • Tipping has not traditionally been part of the Italian culture, as service charge is generally included in the bill, but leaving a few extra coins at the table is often appreciated.
      • Drinking water! There are fountains everywhere you look. They deliver free-flowing, potable water, so save your empty water bottles and fill them up at these fountains.
      • Vegetarian? Look for the Contorni (side dish) section in the menu, it features vegetables of the season.
      • Rome’s public transportation is very good indeed. You can buy bus/ metro tickets at any tabaccheria in the city, little convenience stores that are designated with a large T. Tickets are €1.50 each, or opt for a 24-hour, 48-hour or weekly ticket.
